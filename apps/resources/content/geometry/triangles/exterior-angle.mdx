Push one side of a triangle straight past a corner, like extending a road beyond
an intersection. The angle that opens up *outside* the triangle is an
<Term id="exterior-angle">exterior angle</Term> — and it carries a surprising
piece of information about the two corners far away from it.

## Explore

Reshape the triangle and watch the exterior angle at the extended corner. Keep an
eye on the two **remote** interior angles — the two corners that are *not* next to
it. What relationship holds between the exterior angle and those two?

The exterior angle is **115°** — just $70° + 45°$. The exterior angle always
equals the sum of the two remote interior angles, so you never need the third
(adjacent) angle to find it. Set those two interior angles in the tool and watch
the exterior angle land on $115°$.

## Test it

In the tool, notice two facts holding at once. The exterior angle and the
interior angle right beside it always make a straight line — they are a
[linear pair](/geometry/angles/intersecting-lines), summing to $180°$. And the
exterior angle always equals the two
<Term id="remote-interior-angles">remote interior angles</Term> combined. Try to
find a triangle where either rule fails. You can't.

## Why it works

Both facts come straight from the angle sum. Call the interior angles $A$, $B$,
and $C$, and put the exterior angle at $C$. The exterior angle and $C$ form a
linear pair, so:

$$
\text{exterior} = 180° - C
$$

But the three interior angles also total $180°$, so $180° - C = A + B$. Putting
those together:

$$
\text{exterior} = 180° - C = A + B
$$

The "$180° - C$" cancels out the adjacent angle and leaves exactly the two remote
ones. In the figure that is $180° - 60° = 120°$, matching $70° + 50°$.

<Takeaway>

An exterior angle of a triangle equals the **sum of the two remote interior
angles**. (Equivalently, it is $180°$ minus the interior angle right next to it.)
Because it adds two positive angles, an exterior angle is always *larger* than
either remote interior angle on its own.

</Takeaway>
