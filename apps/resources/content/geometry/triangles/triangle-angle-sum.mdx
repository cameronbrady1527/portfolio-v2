Stretch a triangle however you like — tall and thin, wide and flat, a perfect
equilateral. Its three corners never agree on their sizes, but they always agree
on one thing: their measures **add up to $180°$**, a straight line's worth of
turn.

## Explore

Drag a vertex or the side handles to reshape the triangle. Watch the three angle
measures change — and watch their **sum**. Try to make a triangle whose angles
*don't* total 180°.

## Make a prediction

<PredictThenCheck
  prompt="A triangle has two angles measuring 40° and 75°. What is the third angle?"
  choices={["65°", "115°", "75°", "It depends on the triangle's size"]}
>

The third angle is **65°**. The three must total $180°$, so the last one is
$180° - 40° - 75° = 65°$. Size never enters into it — a tiny triangle and a huge
one with the same two angles have the same third angle. Set two angles in the
tool above and confirm the third lands exactly where it must.

</PredictThenCheck>

## Test it

In the tool, push the triangle toward extremes — make one angle nearly $180°$, or
squash it almost flat. The individual angles swing wildly, but their sum holds at
$180°$ every time. That stubborn constant is the **Triangle Angle Sum Theorem**.

## Why it works

Here is the classic see-it-yourself argument: tear the three corners off a paper
triangle and lay them side by side, points touching. They fit together into a
perfectly straight line — no gap, no overlap.

<AngleSumStrip
  caption="The same three angles, torn off and set point-to-point at one spot. Together they sweep out a straight angle: 50° + 60° + 70° = 180°."
/>

Why must they always tile a straight line? Draw a line through the top vertex
**parallel** to the base. The two base angles reappear at the top as
[alternate interior angles](/geometry/angles/parallel-lines-and-a-transversal)
across the parallel lines — and now all three angles of the triangle sit along
that one straight line at the top, summing to $180°$. (The tool's *"Show why
it's 180°"* reveal animates this directly: it rotates two of the angles up to the
third and lands them on a straight line.)

<Takeaway>

The three interior angles of **any** triangle add to $180°$. Know two of them and
the third is forced: it is $180°$ minus the other two.

</Takeaway>
