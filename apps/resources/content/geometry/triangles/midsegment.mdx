Mark the middle of two sides of a triangle and connect those two points. The
short segment you draw — a <Term id="midsegment">midsegment</Term> — lines up with
the third side in a way that never changes: it runs perfectly **parallel** to it,
and it is exactly **half its length**.

The midsegment is **6 cm** — half of the third side. It does not matter how the

## Test it

In the tool, slide the triangle through tall, flat, and lopsided shapes. The
midsegment and the third side stay **parallel** — same tilt — the whole time, and
the midsegment's length stays glued to exactly half. This pair of facts is the
**Triangle Midsegment Theorem**.

## Why it works

Because the midsegment is parallel to the third side, it cuts the top of the
triangle into a smaller triangle with the *same angles* as the whole — the
parallel line makes equal
[corresponding angles](/geometry/angles/parallel-lines-and-a-transversal). That
smaller triangle is a scaled-down copy of the big one. Each of its two sides runs
from a vertex to a **midpoint**, so it is built at exactly half scale — which
forces its bottom edge, the midsegment, to be half of the matching full side.

<Takeaway>

A **midsegment** joins the midpoints of two sides. It is **parallel** to the
third side and **half its length**: $\text{midsegment} = \tfrac{1}{2}\,(\text{third side})$.

</Takeaway>
