A **dilation** resizes a figure from a fixed point, called the **center of
dilation**, by a **scale factor** $k$. Every point moves along the ray from the
center, so its distance from the center is multiplied by $k$. A dilation must
always be described with *both* pieces: the center and the scale factor.

## Dilating about the origin

When the center is the origin, the rule is simply

$$
(x, y) \mapsto (kx,\, ky)
$$

With $k = 2$, the point $(2, 3)$ lands on $(4, 6)$ — twice as far from the
origin, in the same direction. A factor $k > 1$ **enlarges** the figure; a
factor between $0$ and $1$ **reduces** it.

Watch the side lengths in the figure above as you drag the scale factor: every
length is multiplied by $k$, so with $k = 2$ each side of the image is exactly
twice its preimage side.

## What a dilation preserves

A dilation is **not** a rigid motion: unless $k = 1$, distances change, so the
image is not congruent to the preimage. But the image is always the **same
shape** — every angle keeps its measure — which makes the image **similar** to
the original.

Two more facts you can check experimentally on the figure:

- A line that passes **through the center** of dilation maps onto itself.
- A line that does **not** pass through the center maps to a **parallel**
  line.
