A shape has **symmetry** when some <Term id="rigid-motion">rigid motion</Term> —
other than just leaving it alone — carries it **exactly onto itself**. There are
two kinds to hunt for:

- **<Term id="rotation-symmetry">Rotation symmetry</Term>:** spinning the shape
  about its center by some angle less than $360^\circ$ lands it back on itself.
- **<Term id="line-of-symmetry">Line (reflection) symmetry</Term>:** flipping
  the shape across some line through its center lands it back on itself.

## Hunt: the square

A square has **7**: three nontrivial rotations (90°, 180°, and 270° about its
center) and four lines of symmetry (the two diagonals plus the horizontal and
vertical lines through the center). Knowing the count is the easy part — now
hunt down *which* moves they are.

## Counting symmetries

For a **regular polygon** with $n$ sides, the pattern is beautiful: $n - 1$
rotations (by multiples of $360^\circ / n$) and $n$ lines of symmetry. The
square fits perfectly: $3$ rotations $+$ $4$ lines $= 7$ symmetries.

Irregular shapes break the pattern in interesting ways — a rectangle keeps
only the half-turn and two of the four lines, and a generic parallelogram
keeps the half-turn but **no** lines at all.
