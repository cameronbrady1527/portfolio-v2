One transformation moves a figure once. A **sequence of transformations**
applies several moves **in order**: the image of the first step becomes the
preimage of the second, and so on. Use **Next step** on the figure above to
play the sequence — each earlier position stays behind as a faded ghost so you
can trace the whole journey.

## Order matters

Try this with the point $(1, 2)$:

- **Translate by $\langle 2, 0 \rangle$, then rotate $90^\circ$:**
  $(1, 2) \mapsto (3, 2) \mapsto (-2, 3)$
- **Rotate $90^\circ$, then translate by $\langle 2, 0 \rangle$:**
  $(1, 2) \mapsto (-2, 1) \mapsto (0, 1)$

Same two moves, different order — different result. In the language of
functions, composition of transformations is **not commutative** in general.

## Sequences and congruence

If every step of a sequence is a **rigid motion** (a translation, reflection,
or rotation), then distances and angles survive every step, so the final image
is **congruent** to the original figure. In fact, that is the definition:
two figures are congruent exactly when some sequence of rigid motions carries
one onto the other.

Sneak one **dilation** into the sequence, though, and lengths get scaled —
the final image is **similar** to the original instead.
