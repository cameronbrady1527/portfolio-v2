One transformation moves a figure once. A **sequence of transformations**
applies several moves **in order**: the <Term id="image">image</Term> of the
first step becomes the <Term id="preimage">preimage</Term> of the second, and so
on. Use **Next step** on the figure above to play the sequence — each earlier
position stays behind as a faded ghost so you can trace the whole journey.

  <p>
    Same two moves, different order, different landing spot. In the language of
    functions, composition of transformations is <strong>not commutative</strong>
    in general — and rigid motions are no exception.
  </p>
</PredictThenCheck>

Why should swapping the order change anything? The rotation pivots about a fixed
point — the origin — so where a figure *is* when you spin it changes where it
lands. Translate first and you spin from a new spot; spin first and you slide a
figure that's already turned.

## Sequences and congruence

If every step of a sequence is a <Term id="rigid-motion">rigid motion</Term> (a
translation, reflection, or rotation), then distances and angles survive every
step, so the final image is <Term id="congruent">congruent</Term> to the
original figure. In fact, that is the definition: two figures are congruent
exactly when some sequence of rigid motions carries one onto the other.

Sneak one **dilation** into the sequence, though, and lengths get scaled — the
final image is <Term id="similar">similar</Term> to the original instead.
