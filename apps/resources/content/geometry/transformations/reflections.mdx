A **reflection** is a transformation that flips a figure across a line, called
the <Term id="line-of-reflection">line of reflection</Term>. The original
figure and its <Term id="image">image</Term> are mirror images:
every point moves to the opposite side of the line, the same distance away.

## Reflecting over the axes

<PredictThenCheck
  prompt="The point (3, 1) is reflected over the y-axis. Where does its image land?"
  choices={["(−3, 1)", "(3, −1)", "(−3, −1)", "(1, 3)"]}
>

The image is **(−3, 1)**. The y-axis acts as the mirror, so the point hops to
the other side: the x-coordinate flips sign, and the y-coordinate doesn't move.
Try it on the grapher below — reflect over the y-axis and watch which coordinate
changes.

</PredictThenCheck>

Reflecting a point over the **x-axis** keeps its $x$-coordinate and negates its
$y$-coordinate:

$$
(x, y) \mapsto (x, -y)
$$

Reflecting over the **y-axis** does the opposite — it negates the $x$-coordinate
and keeps $y$:

$$
(x, y) \mapsto (-x, y)
$$

Because a reflection preserves distances and angles, the image is{" "}
<Term id="congruent">congruent</Term> to the original. So a segment $\overline{AB}$ and its image $\overline{A'B'}$
always have the same length.
