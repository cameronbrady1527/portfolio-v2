A **rotation** is a transformation that turns a figure about a fixed point,
called the **center of rotation**, through a given **angle**. Every point of the
figure travels along a circular arc centered at that point, so the distance from
each point to the center never changes. Counter-clockwise angles are taken as
positive, following the standard mathematical convention.

## Rotating about the origin

For the common quarter-turns about the origin, a rotation by an angle
$\theta$ has clean coordinate rules. A $90^\circ$ counter-clockwise rotation
sends a point to:

$$
(x, y) \mapsto (-y,\, x)
$$

The half-turn $180^\circ$ sends $(x, y) \mapsto (-x, -y)$, and the three-quarter
turn $270^\circ$ (equivalently $-90^\circ$) sends $(x, y) \mapsto (y, -x)$.

## Rotating about any center

To rotate about an arbitrary center $(a, b)$ instead of the origin, first
**translate** the figure so the center sits at the origin, apply the rotation
above, then translate back. Because a rotation preserves distances and angles,
the image is always **congruent** to the original figure.
