A line that cuts across two other lines is a <Term id="transversal">transversal</Term>.
It makes a set of angles at each crossing. When the two lines it cuts are
**parallel**, those angles line up in predictable ways — and when the lines are
*not* parallel, the pattern breaks. That break is the whole point: these angle
relationships are a test for parallelism.

## Test it

In the tool, keep "corresponding" selected and flip the lines to **not
parallel**. Do the two corresponding angles stay equal? Now try to find any
non-parallel tilt where a corresponding pair lands back on equal. You can't — the
angles match *only* when the lines are parallel. That is why a surveyor or a
carpenter can use equal corresponding angles to *prove* two lines are parallel.

## Why it works

When the two lines are parallel, four relationships fall out of a single idea —
matching corners agree.

**Corresponding angles** (matching corners) are congruent.

<TransversalPattern relationship="corresponding" />

**Alternate interior angles** — between the lines, on opposite sides of the
transversal, the "Z" pattern — are congruent.

<TransversalPattern relationship="alternate-interior" />

**Same-side interior angles** — between the lines, on the same side of the
transversal, the "C" pattern — are supplementary (they add to $180°$).

<TransversalPattern relationship="same-side-interior" />

Each relationship follows from the one before using the rules from
[intersecting lines](/geometry/angles/intersecting-lines). Start with one
<Term id="corresponding-angles">corresponding</Term> angle. Its
<Term id="alternate-interior-angles">alternate interior</Term> partner is the
*vertical angle* of that corresponding angle, so it is equal too. And a
<Term id="same-side-interior-angles">same-side interior</Term> angle is the
*linear pair* of an alternate interior angle, so it must be its supplement:

$$
\text{same-side interior} = 180° - \text{alternate interior}
$$

<Takeaway>

With parallel lines, **corresponding** and **alternate interior** angles are
congruent, while **same-side interior** angles are supplementary. Every one of
these runs both ways: if the angles match (or sum to 180°), the lines *must* be
parallel.

</Takeaway>
