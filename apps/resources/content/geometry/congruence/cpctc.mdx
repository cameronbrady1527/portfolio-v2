Once you know two triangles are congruent, you get a whole list of equal parts for
free. That is what <Term id="cpctc">CPCTC</Term> stands for: **Corresponding Parts
of Congruent Triangles are Congruent**. If one triangle can be carried exactly onto
the other, then each side lands on a matching side and each angle lands on a
matching angle — so all six pairs are equal.

The one new skill is **reading the correspondence**. A congruence statement lists
its vertices in matching order on purpose:

$$\triangle ABC \cong \triangle DEF \quad\Longrightarrow\quad A\leftrightarrow D,\ \ B\leftrightarrow E,\ \ C\leftrightarrow F.$$

From that single line you can read off every equal part — pair the **letters in the
same position**, never the parts that happen to look closest on the page.

## Why the order matters

CPCTC does not ask you to re-prove anything — the triangles are *already* congruent.
It asks you to **name the right partner**. The trap is matching by appearance: a
reflected triangle can put $E$ where you expect $D$, so a part that *looks* like it
pairs with $AB$ may really be $EF$. Reading straight from $\triangle ABC \cong
\triangle DEF$ keeps you right every time:

- Sides: $AB \cong DE$, $\;BC \cong EF$, $\;CA \cong FD$.
- Angles: $\angle A \cong \angle D$, $\;\angle B \cong \angle E$, $\;\angle C \cong \angle F$.

<Takeaway>

**CPCTC**: if $\triangle ABC \cong \triangle DEF$, then every corresponding pair is
congruent. Find a partner by pairing the **letters in the same position** of the
congruence statement — not by which parts sit closest on the diagram.

</Takeaway>

## Using CPCTC in a proof

CPCTC is almost always the **last line** of a proof. The strategy is a fixed
two-step move:

1. **Get a triangle congruence.** Find two triangles that contain the parts you
   care about, and prove them congruent by one of the criteria — SSS, SAS, ASA,
   AAS, or HL.
2. **Harvest a part with CPCTC.** Once the triangles are congruent, the specific
   side or angle you actually wanted is congruent *because* corresponding parts of
   congruent triangles are congruent.

So before you can write CPCTC, you have to choose the **right criterion** from the
given parts. That single decision — *which three parts are given, and what do they
spell?* — is the whole game.

## Is it congruent? — the checker

Practice the criterion-choosing move on five marked pairs. Four are genuine
congruences; **one is a deliberate trap**. The verdict comes straight from the
figure's coordinates, so it cannot drift from the math.

<PredictThenCheck

**No.** SSA — two sides and a *non-included* angle — is one of the two famous
non-criteria. The marked angle leaves the third side free to swing to **two**
different landing points, so the same three given parts fit two genuinely
different triangles. The checker reports **Not congruent** for that pair because
the two triangles really do not coincide. Valid shortcuts are **SSS, SAS, ASA,
AAS, HL**; the impostors are **SSA** and **AAA**.

</PredictThenCheck>

## Three worked proofs

Each proof makes the same two decisions — *which criterion?* then *what does CPCTC
give?* Predict each before you reveal the finished two-column proof.

### Proof 1 — SSS, then CPCTC

**Given:** $AB \cong CB$, and $M$ is the midpoint of $\overline{AC}$.
**Prove:** $\angle A \cong \angle C$.

The two triangles to compare are $\triangle ABM$ and $\triangle CBM$ — they share
the segment $BM$, and the midpoint splits $AC$ into two equal halves.

<PredictThenCheck
  prompt="Which criterion proves △ABM ≅ △CBM?"
  choices={["SSS", "SAS", "ASA", "AAS"]}
>

**SSS.** List the three pairs of sides: $AB \cong CB$ is given; $AM \cong CM$
because $M$ is the midpoint of $\overline{AC}$; and $BM \cong BM$ by the reflexive
property (it is the *same* segment in both triangles). Three pairs of congruent
sides — that is **SSS**. No angle information is needed.

</PredictThenCheck>

<PredictThenCheck
  prompt="Now that △ABM ≅ △CBM, what does CPCTC let you conclude?"
  choices={["∠A ≅ ∠C", "∠A ≅ ∠B", "AM ≅ BM", "Nothing more"]}
>

**$\angle A \cong \angle C$.** Under $\triangle ABM \cong \triangle CBM$ the first
letters $A$ and $C$ correspond, so $\angle A$ and $\angle C$ are corresponding
angles — and corresponding parts of congruent triangles are congruent. (This is
exactly the proof that the **base angles of an isosceles triangle are equal**.)

</PredictThenCheck>

Now build it: each statement is given in order — supply the reason that justifies it.

### Proof 2 — SAS, then CPCTC

**Given:** $\overline{AC}$ and $\overline{BD}$ bisect each other at $E$.
**Prove:** $AB \cong CD$.

"Bisect each other" means $E$ is the midpoint of *both* diagonals, so it splits
each into two equal pieces. Compare $\triangle AEB$ and $\triangle CED$.

<PredictThenCheck
  prompt="Which criterion proves △AEB ≅ △CED?"
  choices={["SSS", "SAS", "ASA", "HL"]}
>

**SAS.** The bisected diagonals give two pairs of sides: $AE \cong CE$ and
$BE \cong DE$. The angle *between* those sides at $E$ — $\angle AEB$ and
$\angle CED$ — are **vertical angles**, hence congruent. Two sides and the
**included** angle: **SAS**.

</PredictThenCheck>

<PredictThenCheck
  prompt="Once △AEB ≅ △CED, what does CPCTC give you?"
  choices={["AB ≅ CD", "AE ≅ BE", "∠B ≅ ∠C", "Nothing more"]}
>

**$AB \cong CD$.** Under $\triangle AEB \cong \triangle CED$ the correspondence is
$A\leftrightarrow C$, $B\leftrightarrow D$, so the side $AB$ corresponds to $CD$ —
and CPCTC makes corresponding sides congruent.

</PredictThenCheck>

### Proof 3 — ASA, then CPCTC

**Given:** $AB \parallel CD$ and $AD \parallel BC$.
**Prove:** $AB \cong CD$.

Two pairs of parallel sides make $ABCD$ a parallelogram. Draw the diagonal
$\overline{AC}$ and compare $\triangle ABC$ with $\triangle CDA$.

<PredictThenCheck
  prompt="Which criterion proves △ABC ≅ △CDA?"
  choices={["SSS", "SAS", "ASA", "SSA"]}
>

**ASA.** The diagonal $AC$ is a transversal of *both* pairs of parallel lines, so
it creates two pairs of **alternate interior angles**: $\angle BAC \cong \angle
DCA$ (from $AB \parallel CD$) and $\angle BCA \cong \angle DAC$ (from $AD \parallel
BC$). The side $AC \cong AC$ sits **between** those angles by the reflexive
property. Two angles and the **included** side: **ASA**.

</PredictThenCheck>

<PredictThenCheck
  prompt="With △ABC ≅ △CDA established, what does CPCTC conclude?"
  choices={["AB ≅ CD", "AC ≅ BD", "∠B ≅ ∠A", "Nothing more"]}
>

**$AB \cong CD$.** Under $\triangle ABC \cong \triangle CDA$ the correspondence is
$A\leftrightarrow C$, $B\leftrightarrow D$, $C\leftrightarrow A$, so $AB$
corresponds to $CD$. CPCTC gives $AB \cong CD$ — this is the proof that
**opposite sides of a parallelogram are congruent**.

</PredictThenCheck>

<CpctcProof id="asa-parallelogram" />

<Takeaway>

To prove two parts congruent, **find two triangles that contain them, prove the
triangles congruent by a criterion, then finish with CPCTC**. The criterion you
pick is dictated by the given parts: three sides → SSS, two sides and the included
angle → SAS, two angles and the included side → ASA. These three proofs are the
classic results behind isosceles base angles, bisected diagonals, and the opposite
sides of a parallelogram.

</Takeaway>
