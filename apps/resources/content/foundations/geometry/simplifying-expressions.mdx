Before you solve an equation or read a formula, it helps to **simplify** the
expressions inside it. Two moves do almost all the work: **combining like terms**
(adding the pieces that match) and the **distributive property** (clearing
parentheses). Master both and longer problems stop looking intimidating — they
just need tidying first.

<SkillCard skill="simplifying-expressions" title="Simplifying Expressions">

<SkillStep title="What & why">

**Like terms** have the exact same variable part. $3x$ and $2x$ are like terms;
$5$ and $7$ are like terms; but $3x$ and $5$ are *not* — you can't combine them.
Combining like terms just means adding their coefficients: $3x + 2x = 5x$, while
the lone numbers add separately.

The **distributive property** handles parentheses: $a(b + c) = ab + ac$. The
outside factor multiplies *every* term inside — both of them, not just the first.
That's the most common slip, so watch it: $3(x + 4) = 3x + 12$, not $3x + 4$.

</SkillStep>

<SkillStep title="Watch how it works">

**Combine like terms** — match them up, add separately:

$$3x + 5 + 2x = (3x + 2x) + 5 = 5x + 5$$

The $5$ has no like term, so it just rides along.

**Distribute** — multiply the outside number into each term:

$$3(2x + 4) = 3 \cdot 2x + 3 \cdot 4 = 6x + 12$$

**Both together** — distribute first, then combine:

$$2(x + 3) + 4x = 2x + 6 + 4x = 6x + 6$$

The result $6x + 6$ is fully simplified: one $x$-term, one number, nothing left to
combine.

</SkillStep>

<SkillStep title="Reason through one together">

Distribute the factor to *every* term inside the parentheses. Predict before you
check.

<PredictThenCheck
  prompt="What is 3(x + 5) expanded?"
  choices={["3x + 15", "3x + 5", "x + 15", "3x + 8"]}
>

It's **3x + 15**. The $3$ multiplies *both* terms inside: $3 \cdot x = 3x$ and
$3 \cdot 5 = 15$. Leaving the $5$ alone (the $3x + 5$ trap) is the classic
distribution error — the factor has to reach every term in the parentheses.

</PredictThenCheck>

</SkillStep>
