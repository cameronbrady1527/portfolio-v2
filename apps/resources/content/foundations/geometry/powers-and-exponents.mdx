An **exponent** is shorthand for repeated multiplication, and it's everywhere in
geometry — area uses squares, volume uses cubes, the Pythagorean theorem squares
the sides. The skill is small but precise: know exactly what a power means, and
handle negative bases without slipping a sign. This card locks that in.

<SkillCard skill="powers-and-exponents" title="Powers & Exponents">

<SkillStep title="What & why">

A power like $3^4$ has a **base** ($3$) and an **exponent** ($4$). The exponent
counts how many times the base is multiplied by itself:

$$3^4 = 3 \times 3 \times 3 \times 3 = 81$$

The most common mistake is multiplying the base *by* the exponent — $3^4$ is **not**
$3 \times 4 = 12$. It's four factors of $3$.

Two squares to keep straight with **negative bases**: a negative to an *even*
power is positive (the negatives pair up and cancel), and to an *odd* power stays
negative. Parentheses make this visible — $(-2)^4$ means $(-2)$ used four times.

</SkillStep>

<SkillStep title="Watch how it works">

**Build up one factor at a time.** For $2^5$, double five times:

$$2^5 = 2 \times 2 \times 2 \times 2 \times 2 = 32$$

**Powers of ten** are just a $1$ followed by that many zeros — $10^3 = 1000$.

**Negative bases — count the signs.** An even exponent pairs the negatives off:

$$(-3)^2 = (-3)(-3) = 9$$

An odd exponent leaves one negative behind:

$$(-2)^3 = (-2)(-2)(-2) = -8$$

</SkillStep>

<SkillStep title="Reason through one together">

A negative base is where signs go wrong. Count how many negatives you have, then
decide the sign. Predict before you check.

<PredictThenCheck
  prompt="Evaluate (−3)²."
  choices={["9", "−9", "6", "−6"]}
>

It's **9**. $(-3)^2$ means $(-3) \times (-3)$. Two negatives multiply to a
positive, so the result is $+9$. The $-9$ trap comes from squaring only the $3$
and keeping the sign; the $6$ trap comes from doing $3 \times 2$ instead of using
the exponent. Two factors of $-3$ give $9$.

</PredictThenCheck>

</SkillStep>
