When powers share the same base, you can combine them without writing out all the
factors. Three rules cover almost everything: **add** exponents to multiply,
**subtract** to divide, and **multiply** to raise a power to another power. The
trick isn't memorizing them — it's seeing *why* each works and not mixing them up.

<SkillCard skill="exponent-rules" title="Exponent Rules">

<SkillStep title="What & why">

Every exponent rule comes straight from "an exponent counts factors." Multiply
$2^3 \times 2^2$ and you're just stacking factors of $2$:

$$2^3 \times 2^2 = (2 \cdot 2 \cdot 2)(2 \cdot 2) = 2^5$$

Three factors plus two more makes five — so for the **same base**, multiplying
means **adding** exponents. Dividing cancels factors, so it means **subtracting**.
And a power of a power, like $(2^3)^2$, is two copies of $2^3$ multiplied —
$2^3 \times 2^3 = 2^6$ — so it means **multiplying** the exponents.

The catch that trips everyone: these rules combine the **exponents**, never the
bases. $3^2 \times 3^4 = 3^6$, not $9^6$ — the base stays $3$.

</SkillStep>

<SkillStep title="Watch how it works">

**Product — add the exponents.**

$$3^4 \times 3^2 = 3^{4+2} = 3^6$$

**Quotient — subtract the exponents.**

$$5^7 \div 5^3 = 5^{7-3} = 5^4$$

**Power of a power — multiply the exponents.**

$$(2^3)^2 = 2^{3 \times 2} = 2^6$$

Same base every time; only the exponents combine, by the operation that matches.

</SkillStep>

<SkillStep title="Reason through one together">

Decide which rule applies, then combine the exponents — and keep the base.
Predict before you check.

<PredictThenCheck
  prompt="Write 2³ × 2⁴ as a single power of 2."
  choices={["2⁷", "2¹²", "4⁷", "8⁷"]}
>

It's **2⁷**. Same base, multiplication, so *add* the exponents: $3 + 4 = 7$, giving
$2^7$. The $2^{12}$ trap is multiplying the exponents (that's the power-of-a-power
rule, not this one), and $4^7$ comes from multiplying the bases — but the base
stays $2$.

</PredictThenCheck>

</SkillStep>
