Geometry is full of multiplying and dividing: area is length times width, a figure
scaled by a factor multiplies every side, and splitting a segment into equal parts
is division. When those operations are **automatic**, you get to spend your
attention on the geometry instead of the arithmetic. This card sharpens the facts
and the multi-digit moves until they're sure.

<SkillCard skill="multiplication-division-fluency" title="Multiplication & Division Fluency">

<SkillStep title="What & why">

**Multiplication** is repeated addition — $4 \times 3$ is four groups of three — and
it's also how we *scale*: doubling a length is $\times 2$. **Division** is the
inverse: it splits a total into equal groups, or asks how many groups fit.

They're two sides of one fact. Because $6 \times 8 = 48$, you instantly know
$48 \div 6 = 8$ and $48 \div 8 = 6$. Every division question is really a
multiplication question in disguise: *"this divisor times what gives the
dividend?"* Hold that link and the two operations reinforce each other instead of
being two things to memorize.

</SkillStep>

<SkillStep title="Watch how it works">

**Multi-digit multiplication is just smaller facts, added up.** Break a factor
apart by place value. Take $23 \times 14$:

$$23 \times 14 = (23 \times 10) + (23 \times 4) = 230 + 92 = 322$$

You only ever multiply by single digits and tens — the place-value split does the
rest.

**Division reaches for the matching fact.** Take $144 \div 9$. Don't search blindly;
ask *"$9$ times what makes $144$?"* Since $9 \times 16 = 144$, the answer is $16$.

$$144 \div 9 = 16$$

Same fact family, read in the other direction.

</SkillStep>

<SkillStep title="Reason through one together">

Real problems often hand you a division that doesn't come out even — and then the
*situation* decides what to do with the leftover.

<PredictThenCheck
  prompt="A baker packs 8 muffins per box and has 100 muffins. How many FULL boxes can she fill?"
  choices={["12", "13", "12.5", "8"]}
>

It's **12**. Dividing, $100 \div 8 = 12$ remainder $4$ — twelve full boxes with
$4$ muffins left over. Those $4$ don't fill a box, so the honest answer is $12$,
not $12.5$ and not rounded up to $13$. When you divide in a real context, always
ask what the remainder *means* before you write the answer.

</PredictThenCheck>

</SkillStep>
