Solving an equation means finding the value of the variable that makes it true.
The whole method is one idea: **keep the equation balanced** by doing the same
inverse operation to both sides, peeling away everything around the variable until
it stands alone. It's the other half of the Regents spine (alongside
[substituting into a formula](/foundations/geometry/substituting-into-formulas)),
and you'll use it to find missing sides, angles, and unknowns all course long.

<SkillCard skill="solving-linear-equations" title="Solving Linear Equations">

<SkillStep title="What & why">

An equation is a balance: the left side equals the right side. To keep it true,
**whatever you do to one side you must do to the other.** Solving is undoing the
operations around the variable, in reverse, using **inverse operations** —
addition undoes subtraction, multiplication undoes division.

So to free $x$ from $3x + 5$, first undo the $+5$ (subtract $5$ from both sides),
then undo the $\times 3$ (divide both sides by $3$). Reverse order, inverse
operations, both sides every time. That discipline is the entire skill.

</SkillStep>

<SkillStep title="Watch how it works">

**Two steps, undone in reverse.** Solve $3x + 5 = 20$:

$$3x + 5 = 20 \;\Rightarrow\; 3x = 15 \;\Rightarrow\; x = 5$$

Subtract $5$ from both sides, then divide both sides by $3$.

**Variables on both sides?** Gather them first. Solve $5x + 2 = 3x + 10$ by
subtracting $3x$ from both sides:

$$5x + 2 = 3x + 10 \;\Rightarrow\; 2x + 2 = 10 \;\Rightarrow\; 2x = 8 \;\Rightarrow\; x = 4$$

**Parentheses?** [Distribute](/foundations/geometry/simplifying-expressions) or
divide first — $2(x + 4) = 18$ becomes $x + 4 = 9$, so $x = 5$. You can always
**check** by substituting your answer back in.

</SkillStep>

<SkillStep title="Reason through one together">

Undo the operations in reverse, one at a time, on both sides. Predict before you
check.

<PredictThenCheck
  prompt="Solve for x:  2x + 5 = 13"
  choices={["4", "9", "6", "3"]}
>

It's **4**. First undo the $+5$: subtract $5$ from both sides to get $2x = 8$.
Then undo the $\times 2$: divide both sides by $2$ to get $x = 4$. Check:
$2(4) + 5 = 8 + 5 = 13$. ✓ Dividing before subtracting (a tempting shortcut) would
break the balance — peel off the $+5$ first.

</PredictThenCheck>

</SkillStep>
