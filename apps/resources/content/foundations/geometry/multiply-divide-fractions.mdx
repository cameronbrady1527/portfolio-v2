Multiplying and dividing fractions is, surprisingly, *easier* than adding them —
no common denominator needed. Multiplication is taking "a part of a part" (and
the word **of** is your signal), and division asks "how many of these fit into
that." Each has one clean procedure. This card makes both automatic.

<SkillCard skill="multiply-divide-fractions" title="Multiplying & Dividing Fractions">

<SkillStep title="What & why">

**Multiplying** a fraction by a fraction means taking a part *of* a part. Half
*of* a half is a quarter: $\frac{1}{2} \times \frac{1}{2} = \frac{1}{4}$. That's
why "of" in a problem so often means multiply.

**Dividing** asks how many of one fraction fit into another — how many
$\frac{1}{4}$-cups fit in $\frac{1}{2}$ a cup? The trick is that dividing by a
fraction is the same as **multiplying by its reciprocal** (its flip). Both
operations come down to multiplying, so neither needs a common denominator.

</SkillStep>

<SkillStep title="Watch how it works">

**Multiply straight across** — tops together, bottoms together — then simplify:

$$\frac{2}{3} \times \frac{3}{4} = \frac{2 \times 3}{3 \times 4} = \frac{6}{12} = \frac{1}{2}$$

**Divide by flipping the second fraction**, then multiply. To divide by
$\frac{1}{2}$, multiply by $\frac{2}{1}$:

$$\frac{3}{4} \div \frac{1}{2} = \frac{3}{4} \times \frac{2}{1} = \frac{6}{4} = \frac{3}{2}$$

That answer being *more than 1* makes sense: more than one half-sized piece fits
into three-quarters. "Flip and multiply" is the whole move for division.

</SkillStep>

<SkillStep title="Reason through one together">

Read division as "how many fit?" and the flip-and-multiply will confirm your
intuition. Predict before you check.

<PredictThenCheck
  prompt="1/2 ÷ 1/4 = ?"
  choices={["2", "1/8", "8", "1/2"]}
>

It's **2**. Picture it: how many quarter-cups fit in half a cup? Two. The
procedure agrees — flip $\frac{1}{4}$ to $\frac{4}{1}$ and multiply:
$\frac{1}{2} \times \frac{4}{1} = \frac{4}{2} = 2$. Multiplying the two fractions
instead (the $\frac{1}{8}$ trap) would *shrink* the amount, but dividing by a
number less than $1$ makes the result *bigger*.

</PredictThenCheck>

</SkillStep>
