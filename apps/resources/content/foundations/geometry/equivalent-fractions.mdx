A fraction names a part of a whole, and the *same* part can be named many ways:
half a pizza is $\frac{1}{2}$, or $\frac{2}{4}$, or $\frac{4}{8}$. Being able to
rewrite a fraction as an equal one — on demand — is the move behind comparing
fractions, adding them, simplifying answers, and working with ratios and scale.
This card makes that move automatic.

<SkillCard skill="equivalent-fractions" title="Equivalent Fractions">

<SkillStep title="What & why">

A fraction is a **division of a whole into equal parts**: the bottom number
(denominator) says how many parts the whole is cut into, and the top
(numerator) says how many you take. So $\frac{3}{4}$ means *cut into 4, take 3*.

Here's the key idea: if you cut every part into the same number of smaller
pieces, you have **more pieces but the same amount**. Cut each of the $4$ parts
in two and you get $8$ parts, and the $3$ you took become $6$ — so
$\frac{3}{4} = \frac{6}{8}$. Nothing about the *amount* changed; only how finely
you sliced it. That's why equivalents matter everywhere in this course: to
compare or add fractions you first re-slice them to a common size.

</SkillStep>

<SkillStep title="Watch how it works">

**Multiply top and bottom by the same number.** To rewrite $\frac{2}{3}$ with a
denominator of $12$, ask what times $3$ gives $12$ — that's $4$ — then do the
same to the top:

$$\frac{2}{3} = \frac{2 \times 4}{3 \times 4} = \frac{8}{12}$$

The two bars below hold the *same* shaded amount; the bottom one is just sliced
into more pieces. The dashed line shows the shaded edges landing in exactly the
same place — that's what "equal" looks like.

<SkillStep title="Reason through one together">

The whole trick is "same factor, top and bottom." Find the factor first, then
apply it. Predict before you check.

<PredictThenCheck
  prompt="Fill in the missing number: 2/3 = ?/12"
  choices={["8", "6", "4", "9"]}
>

It's **8**. The denominator went from $3$ to $12$, which is $\times 4$. To keep
the fraction equal you must multiply the top by the same $4$: $2 \times 4 = 8$,
so $\frac{2}{3} = \frac{8}{12}$. Multiplying only the bottom would change the
amount — it's the *same factor on both* that keeps it equal.

</PredictThenCheck>
