The coordinate plane is where algebra and geometry meet — every shape becomes a
set of points you can measure and transform. It starts with two simple ideas: a
point is named by an **ordered pair** $(x, y)$, and the two axes divide the plane
into four **quadrants**. Get fluent with both and the rest of coordinate geometry
has a place to stand.

<SkillCard skill="points-and-quadrants" title="Points & Quadrants">

<SkillStep title="What & why">

A point's address is an **ordered pair** $(x, y)$: the first number is how far
**right or left** (the $x$-coordinate), the second is how far **up or down** (the
$y$-coordinate). Order matters — $(3, 5)$ and $(5, 3)$ are different places.

The $x$- and $y$-axes cross at the **origin** $(0, 0)$ and cut the plane into four
**quadrants**, numbered counterclockwise from the top right with Roman numerals.
Each has its own sign pattern:

- **Quadrant I** $(+, +)$ · **Quadrant II** $(-, +)$
- **Quadrant III** $(-, -)$ · **Quadrant IV** $(+, -)$

A point *on* an axis (an $x$ or $y$ of $0$) belongs to no quadrant.

</SkillStep>

<SkillStep title="Watch how it works">

Read the signs and the quadrant follows. $(4, 7)$ is right and up — Quadrant I.
$(-5, 3)$ is left and up — Quadrant II.

<CoordinateGrid
  points={[{ x: 4, y: 7 }, { x: -5, y: 3 }]}

  caption="(4, 7) sits in Quadrant I; (−5, 3) in Quadrant II."

The signs are the whole story: a *negative* $x$ pushes you left, a *negative* $y$
pushes you down. Track those two signs and you can name any point's quadrant
without plotting it.

</SkillStep>

<SkillStep title="Reason through one together">

Check each coordinate's sign, then read off the quadrant. Predict before you
check.

<PredictThenCheck
  prompt="Which quadrant contains the point (−4, 6)?"
  choices={["Quadrant II", "Quadrant I", "Quadrant III", "Quadrant IV"]}
>

It's **Quadrant II**. The $x$-coordinate $-4$ is negative (left of the $y$-axis)
and the $y$-coordinate $6$ is positive (above the $x$-axis). Left-and-up is the
top-left region — Quadrant II. The sign pair $(-, +)$ points there directly.

</PredictThenCheck>

</SkillStep>
