Rounding shows up two ways in this course. First as **estimation** — a quick check
that an answer is in the right ballpark before you trust it. Second as **judgment**
— a calculation gives $7.3$ buses, but you can't send $0.3$ of a bus, so the
*situation* tells you to round up to $8$. This card builds both: the place-value
rule, and the real-world sense of which way to go.

<SkillCard skill="rounding" title="Rounding">

<SkillStep title="What & why">

To round a number to a given place, you look at **one digit** — the one
immediately to the *right* of that place — and follow a single rule:

- If it's **5 or more**, round the target digit **up**.
- If it's **4 or less**, leave the target digit and round **down**.

Either way, every digit after the target place becomes $0$. That's the whole
mechanism. Rounding trades exactness for a cleaner, easier-to-reason-about number —
useful for estimating, and essential when a fractional answer makes no sense in the
real world.

</SkillStep>

<SkillStep title="Watch how it works">

**Place value, one digit decides.** Round $3472$ to the nearest hundred. The digit
right of the hundreds place is the tens digit, $7$. Since $7 \ge 5$, the hundreds
round up:

$$3472 \rightarrow 3500$$

Round $832$ to the nearest ten instead: the ones digit is $2$, which is less than
$5$, so it rounds down to $830$.

**Real-world: round to fit the situation.** You have $100$ cans and boxes that hold
$12$. Dividing, $100 \div 12 = 8$ remainder $4$. Eight boxes leave $4$ cans
homeless — so you need a **ninth** box. Here you *must* round up, no matter how
small the remainder, because a leftover still needs a container.

</SkillStep>

<SkillStep title="Reason through one together">

When a question asks "how many do we need to hold everyone," a leftover *always*
costs you one more. Decide before you peek.

<PredictThenCheck
  prompt="Each van holds 9 people. A team of 50 needs rides. How many vans are needed?"
  choices={["6", "5", "5.5", "45"]}
>

It's **6**. Dividing, $50 \div 9 = 5$ remainder $5$ — five vans carry $45$ people,
leaving $5$ still waiting. Those $5$ need a sixth van, so you round **up** to $6$.
Notice you ignore the normal "is it 5 or more" digit rule here: any remainder at
all forces one more, because you can't run a fraction of a van.

</PredictThenCheck>

</SkillStep>
