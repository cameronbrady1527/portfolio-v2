Geometry formulas stack operations together — $\tfrac{1}{2}bh$, $s^2$, $a^2 + b^2$.
Plug numbers in and you get a multi-step expression, and there's exactly **one**
correct value — but only if you evaluate it in the agreed order. This card makes
that order automatic so your formula work is never off by a hidden mis-step.

<SkillCard skill="order-of-operations" title="Order of Operations">

<SkillStep title="What & why">

Read $4 + 3 \times 5$ left to right and you'd get $35$. Multiply first and you get
$19$. Both can't be right — so mathematicians agreed on a fixed order, and it
isn't arbitrary: multiplication is repeated addition, so it "binds tighter" than
the addition around it.

The order is **PEMDAS**:

- **P**arentheses
- **E**xponents
- **M**ultiplication and **D**ivision — together, left to right
- **A**ddition and **S**ubtraction — together, left to right

The pairs matter: M and D are the *same* rank (do them in the order they appear),
and so are A and S. They are not four separate steps but two ranked tiers riding
on grouping and powers.

</SkillStep>

<SkillStep title="Watch how it works">

**Multiplication before addition.** In $4 + 3 \times 5$, the $3 \times 5$ happens
first:

$$4 + 3 \times 5 = 4 + 15 = 19$$

**Parentheses override that.** Wrap the addition and it goes first — same numbers,
different answer:

$$(4 + 3) \times 5 = 7 \times 5 = 35$$

**Exponents come before multiply/add.** In $2 + 3^2$, square first:

$$2 + 3^2 = 2 + 9 = 11$$

Work one operation at a time and **rewrite the whole expression** after each step —
that habit is what keeps long ones from going sideways.

</SkillStep>

<SkillStep title="Reason through one together">

Here's the trap: an operation that comes *later* in reading order can still have to
go *first*. Picture the steps before you choose.

<PredictThenCheck
  prompt="Evaluate 20 − 12 ÷ 4."
  choices={["17", "2", "8", "16"]}
>

It's **17**. Division outranks subtraction, so $12 \div 4 = 3$ happens first,
*then* $20 - 3 = 17$. Going strictly left to right would give $20 - 12 = 8$, then
$8 \div 4 = 2$ — a different, wrong answer. The subtraction is written first, but
it waits its turn.

</PredictThenCheck>

</SkillStep>
