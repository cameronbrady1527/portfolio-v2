The **midpoint** is the point exactly halfway between two others. Finding it is one
of the friendliest moves in coordinate geometry: just **average** the
$x$-coordinates and average the $y$-coordinates. It's the foundation for
perpendicular bisectors, centers of figures, and symmetry — and it builds right on
the [coordinate plane](/foundations/geometry/points-and-quadrants).

<SkillCard skill="midpoint" title="Midpoint">

<SkillStep title="What & why">

Halfway between two numbers is their **average** — halfway between $2$ and $8$ is
$\frac{2 + 8}{2} = 5$. The midpoint of a segment just does this in both directions
at once:

$$M = \left(\frac{x_1 + x_2}{2}, \ \frac{y_1 + y_2}{2}\right)$$

Average the $x$'s for the midpoint's $x$, average the $y$'s for its $y$. The one
trap is **adding without halving** — the sum of the endpoints overshoots the
middle by double.

</SkillStep>

<SkillStep title="Watch how it works">

Take the segment from $(2, 2)$ to $(8, 6)$. Average each coordinate:

$$M = \left(\frac{2 + 8}{2}, \ \frac{2 + 6}{2}\right) = (5, 4)$$

The midpoint $(5, 4)$ lands squarely in the center of the segment:

  points={[{ x: 2, y: 2, label: "A" }, { x: 8, y: 6, label: "B" }, { x: 5, y: 4, label: "M" }]}

Watch the signs when a coordinate is negative: the midpoint of $(-4, 1)$ and
$(6, 9)$ has $x = \frac{-4 + 6}{2} = 1$.

</SkillStep>

<SkillStep title="Reason through one together">

Average the $x$'s and the $y$'s separately — don't forget to halve. Predict before
you check.

<PredictThenCheck
  prompt="What is the midpoint of (2, 2) and (8, 6)?"
  choices={["(5, 4)", "(10, 8)", "(3, 2)", "(6, 4)"]}
>

It's **(5, 4)**. Average each coordinate: $x = \frac{2 + 8}{2} = 5$ and
$y = \frac{2 + 6}{2} = 4$. The $(10, 8)$ trap is adding the endpoints without
dividing by $2$ — that gives a corner of the bounding box, not the center.

</PredictThenCheck>

</SkillStep>
