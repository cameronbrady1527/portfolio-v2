An exponential model has the form $y = a \cdot b^t$. Two numbers tell the whole
story:

- **$a$ — the initial value**: the amount at $t = 0$ (every base raised to the
  $0$ power is $1$).
- **$b$ — the growth or decay factor**: rewrite it as $1 \pm r$ to read the
  **percent rate**. If $b = 1 + r$ it's **growth** (e.g. $1.05 \to 5\%$ up); if
  $b = 1 - r$ it's **decay** (e.g. $0.75 \to 25\%$ down — *not* $75\%$).

This is the companion to **Linear vs. Exponential**: there you decide *which*
model fits; here you read what an exponential model's numbers mean. These are
**real, released Regents questions**.
